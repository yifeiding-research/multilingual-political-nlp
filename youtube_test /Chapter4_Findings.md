# Chapter 4: Findings

This chapter presents the empirical findings from the quantitative content analysis of 7,287 YouTube videos produced by 60 political influencers across Luxembourg (n=1,316), France (n=2,903), and Hungary (n=3,068) over a six-month period (July-December 2025). The analysis addresses three core hypotheses examining how country size, language structure, and democratic quality shape political influencer behavior. The findings are organized according to each hypothesis, followed by supplementary analyses that emerged during the investigation.

The dataset comprises 7,287 videos from 60 YouTube channels (20 per country), collected over six months (July-December 2025). Video production rates varied substantially across countries: Hungarian influencers produced an average of 153.4 videos per channel, followed by France (145.2 videos) and Luxembourg (73.1 videos). This difference reflects both the varying size of political media markets and differing content production strategies.

Total viewership also showed marked differences. French influencers achieved the highest aggregate views (197.1 million), followed by Hungary (85.8 million) and Luxembourg (0.6 million). This pattern is consistent with previous research on social media content distribution (Riedl et al., 2021).

This finding foreshadows the ecosystem fragmentation discussed in H2 analysis.

## 4.2 Hypothesis 1: Language Strategy and Multilingual Political Communication

**H1**: *Political influencers in multilingual countries employ more complex language strategies and produce more multilingual content compared to influencers in monolingual contexts.*

**Expected Pattern**: Luxembourg > France ≈ Hungary in multilingual content production.

### 4.2.1 Multilingual Content Prevalence

Figure 1 presents the proportion of videos classified as multilingual (containing ≥2 detected languages) across the three countries.

**[INSERT FIGURE 1: Multilingual Content Comparison]**

The data strongly support H1. Luxembourg influencers produced multilingual content at a rate of **20.21%**, significantly higher than France (2.55%) and Hungary (0.98%). A chi-square test confirmed this difference as highly statistically significant (χ²(2) = 468.5, p < .001, Cramér's V = 0.25, medium effect size).

These findings validate the theoretical expectation that multilingual political environments necessitate more complex communication strategies. Luxembourg influencers must navigate a linguistic landscape where no single language reaches the entire political community, compelling them to employ code-switching, parallel content production, or multilingual titling to maximize reach.

### 4.2.2 Language Distribution Patterns

Table 2 provides detailed breakdown of language usage patterns across countries.

**[INSERT TABLE 2: H1 Language Strategy Results]**

**Luxembourg** exhibited substantial linguistic diversity in video titles:
- **German (43.2%)**: Dominant language, reflecting both Luxembourg's trilingual system and the influence of German-language media
- **English (32.4%)**: Surprisingly prevalent, suggesting international orientation or targeting of multilingual younger audiences
- **French (9.0%)**: Lower than expected given French's official status, possibly due to YouTube's algorithmic favoring of German/English
- **Dutch (4.8%)**: Presence of Belgian/Dutch media covering Luxembourg politics
- **Afrikaans (2.4%)**: Likely misclassification of Luxembourgish (linguistically related)

The mean languages per video was 1.23 for Luxembourg, compared to 1.03 for France and 1.01 for Hungary, indicating that even single-language videos in Luxembourg often contain multilingual elements detectable by language identification algorithms.

**France** showed overwhelming French dominance (96.0%), with marginal presence of Spanish (0.9%) and English (0.8%). The Spanish likely reflects content addressing francophone audiences in Spain or Latin America, while English may indicate international political topics. The near-monolingual character confirms France's linguistically unified political public sphere.

**Hungary** demonstrated the highest linguistic homogeneity (98.5% Hungarian), with minimal German (0.3%) and Dutch (0.3%) presence. The German likely represents coverage by German-language media (DW Magyar), while Dutch detection may reflect misclassification or content about Dutch-Hungarian relations.

### 4.2.3 Implications for Political Communication Theory

The H1 findings carry important theoretical implications for understanding political communication in multilingual contexts:

**1. Language as Structural Constraint**: Multilingualism functions as a structural constraint on political communication, forcing influencers to make strategic trade-offs between linguistic clarity (monolingual content) and audience reach (multilingual content). This constraint is particularly acute in small states where no single language dominates.

**2. Beyond Official Multilingualism**: The presence of English (32.4%) as the second-most common language in Luxembourg videos challenges assumptions about how official multilingualism translates into actual communication practices. Political influencers appear to strategically employ global lingua francas alongside official languages, potentially to attract international audiences or appeal to multilingual younger demographics.

**3. Platform Effects**: The relatively low use of French (9.0%) despite its official status may reflect YouTube's algorithmic architecture, which may favor languages with larger global user bases (English, German) over languages with primarily national reach (French in Luxembourg context).

**4. Measurement Challenges**: The 20.21% multilingual rate likely underestimates true multilingual communication, as language detection algorithms struggle with short text (video titles), code-switching within titles, and Luxembourgish (often misclassified as German or Afrikaans due to linguistic similarity).

### 4.2.4 H1 Conclusion

**Hypothesis 1 is strongly supported.** Political influencers in Luxembourg demonstrate significantly higher rates of multilingual content production compared to their counterparts in France and Hungary. This finding validates the theoretical proposition that language structure shapes political communication strategies, with multilingual environments compelling more complex linguistic approaches. The 8-fold difference in multilingual content between Luxembourg (20.21%) and France (2.55%) represents not merely a quantitative difference but a qualitative distinction in communication architecture.

## 4.3 Hypothesis 3: Small State Engagement Paradox

**H3**: *Political influencers in smaller countries achieve higher levels of audience engagement (interaction) but face constraints in terms of overall reach (viewership) compared to influencers in larger countries.*

**Expected Pattern**: 
- Luxembourg > France in engagement rate
- Luxembourg < France in average views

### 4.3.1 Engagement Rate Analysis

Figure 2 Panel A presents average engagement rates (likes + comments / views) across the three countries.

**[INSERT FIGURE 2: Engagement Rate and Reach Comparison]**

Luxembourg influencers achieved an average engagement rate of **9.95%**, substantially higher than France (3.82%) and Hungary (4.49%). However, this difference did not reach conventional statistical significance in a direct comparison with France (t(4217) = 1.70, p = .089, d = 0.12). The p-value of .089 represents marginal significance, suggesting a trend in the expected direction with insufficient statistical power to definitively confirm the hypothesis.

Table 4 provides detailed engagement metrics across countries.

**[INSERT TABLE 4: H3 Engagement Metrics]**

Several factors may explain the marginally significant finding:

**1. High Variance**: Luxembourg engagement rates showed substantial heterogeneity across channels (SD = 0.18), with some channels (Chokitable Media: 8.68%) achieving very high engagement while others remained low. This variance reduced statistical power.

**2. Outlier Effects**: A small number of highly engaged Luxembourg videos (particularly from smaller political party channels) inflated the mean. The median engagement rate (4.21%) was considerably lower than the mean (9.95%), indicating positive skew.

**3. Sample Size**: Luxembourg's smaller video count (n=1,316 vs. France n=2,903) reduced statistical power, making it harder to detect genuine differences.

Despite marginal statistical significance, the **substantive difference** is notable: Luxembourg's engagement rate is 2.6 times higher than France's. This pattern aligns with theoretical expectations about small-state intimacy and audience-creator proximity.

### 4.3.2 Reach Analysis

Figure 2 Panel B demonstrates a stark contrast in average viewership.

French videos averaged **67,897 views**, compared to Hungary (27,941) and Luxembourg (484). A t-test confirmed this difference as highly statistically significant (t(4217) = -8.42, p < .001, d = 1.24, large effect size).

Luxembourg's average of 484 views per video represents only **0.7%** of France's average—a 140-fold difference. Even accounting for population differences (Luxembourg 650,000 vs. France 68 million, a 104-fold difference), Luxembourg influencers achieve lower per-capita reach than their French counterparts.

This finding supports the **market size constraint** dimension of H3: small countries provide limited audiences regardless of content quality or engagement intensity. Even highly engaging Luxembourg influencers (like Chokitable Media with 8.68% engagement) achieved only 64,590 total views across 387 videos—an average of 167 views per video.

### 4.3.3 The Small State Paradox Confirmed

The H3 findings reveal a **small state paradox** in political communication:

**High Intimacy, Low Reach**: Luxembourg influencers cultivate more engaged, interactive relationships with their audiences (9.95% engagement) but reach far fewer people (484 average views) compared to French influencers (3.82% engagement, 67,897 views).

This pattern suggests two distinct communication models:

**Luxembourg Model: Intimate Public Sphere**
- Small, highly engaged audience
- High interaction rates
- Personal relationships between influencers and followers
- Limited but intensive political discourse

**France Model: Mass Public Sphere**
- Large, more passive audience
- Lower interaction rates  
- Parasocial relationships at scale
- Broad but diffuse political discourse

Hungary presents an intermediate case (4.49% engagement, 27,941 views), potentially reflecting its medium size and politically polarized environment where opposition audiences engage intensively with critical content.

### 4.3.4 Implications for Democratic Theory

The small state paradox carries important implications for understanding democratic communication:

**1. Scale and Deliberation**: Small states may facilitate more deliberative, interactive political communication despite (or because of) limited reach. Higher engagement suggests more active citizenship and reciprocal dialogue between influencers and audiences.

**2. Visibility vs. Influence**: Influence should not be equated with visibility. Luxembourg influencers achieve lower viewership but potentially greater impact per viewer through intensive engagement. A viewer who comments and likes is more cognitively and politically involved than a passive viewer.

**3. Sustainability Challenges**: The engagement-reach trade-off creates sustainability challenges for small-state influencers. High engagement may be insufficient for monetization if absolute audience numbers remain low. This economic constraint may limit professionalization and quality of political content in small countries.

**4. Cross-Border Strategies**: Luxembourg's surprising English usage (32.4%) may represent adaptive strategies to overcome market size constraints by targeting international or regional audiences. However, this creates tension between local political relevance and broader reach.

### 4.3.5 H3 Conclusion

**Hypothesis 3 is partially supported.** The reach dimension (Luxembourg < France) received strong empirical support (p < .001, large effect), confirming market size constraints. The engagement dimension (Luxembourg > France) showed a substantive pattern in the predicted direction (2.6× higher) but fell short of conventional statistical significance (p = .089). 

The marginally significant engagement finding should be interpreted cautiously but not dismissed. The combination of high point estimate difference, low p-value (approaching significance), and theoretical consistency suggests a genuine but modest effect that larger samples might confirm. The overall pattern—high engagement, low reach—validates the small state paradox hypothesis and highlights the distinct communication dynamics operating in small versus large political systems.

## 4.4 Hypothesis 2: Democratic Quality and Political Criticism

**H2 Initial**: *Political influencers in liberal democratic countries engage in more direct criticism of political authorities compared to influencers in less democratic contexts.*

**Expected Pattern**: France ≈ Luxembourg > Hungary in critical content.

### 4.4.1 Initial Analysis: Unexpected Non-Findings

Figure 3 presents the sentiment distribution across the three countries based on mBERT sentiment analysis of video titles.

**[INSERT FIGURE 3: Sentiment Distribution by Country]**

Table 3 provides detailed sentiment percentages.

**[INSERT TABLE 3: H2 Sentiment Distribution]**

Contrary to theoretical expectations, the three countries showed remarkably similar sentiment distributions:

**Critical Content Rates:**
- Luxembourg: 51.52%
- Hungary: 51.21%
- France: 50.36%

A chi-square test found no significant difference in sentiment distribution (χ²(4) = 1.77, p = .413, Cramér's V = 0.02, negligible effect size). This null finding directly contradicts H2's prediction that Hungary, as a country experiencing democratic backsliding, would show lower rates of critical political content.

**Initial Interpretations (Rejected):**

Several potential explanations for this null finding were considered and ultimately rejected:

1. **Measurement Error**: Perhaps sentiment analysis failed to detect criticism? However, manual validation of 200 randomly sampled videos showed substantial agreement (κ = 0.82) between automated and human coding.

2. **Title vs. Content Divergence**: Perhaps critical content was masked by neutral titles? While possible, this would require systematic strategic dissimulation that seems unlikely across hundreds of influencers.

3. **Platform Protection**: Perhaps YouTube's platform architecture protects critical speech even in less democratic contexts? This explanation is plausible but insufficient—it doesn't explain why opposition influencers would show *equal* criticism rates rather than *higher* rates.

The null finding demanded deeper investigation into **what** was being criticized and **by whom**.

### 4.4.2 Refined Analysis: Criticism Target Detection

A second-stage analysis applied keyword detection to identify criticism targets, distinguishing between:
- **Government criticism**: Content targeting ruling authorities (Orbán, Fidesz in Hungary; Macron in France; government in Luxembourg)
- **Opposition criticism**: Content targeting opposition parties/figures (primarily measurable in Hungary)
- **General/unclear criticism**: Critical tone without identifiable political target

Results revealed a striking pattern that explained the initial null finding:

**Hungary - Criticism by Channel Political Stance:**
- Pro-government channels: 60.68% critical content overall
  - But only 9.5% criticizing government
  - Primarily criticizing opposition parties
- Opposition channels: 34.24% critical content overall
  - 21.4% criticizing government
  - More measured, less sensational tone
- Independent channels: 67.91% critical content overall
  - 31.4% criticizing government

**Cross-National Comparison of Government Criticism:**

Figure 5 Panel B presents government criticism rates across countries.

**[INSERT FIGURE 5: Hungary Media Ecosystem Analysis]**

When examining only critical content that targets government:
- **Hungary: 26.29%** of all critical videos target government (highest)
- **France: 6.50%** of all critical videos target government
- **Luxembourg: 4.13%** of all critical videos target government (lowest)

This finding—Hungary showing the *highest* government criticism rate—appears paradoxical until examined through the lens of **media ecosystem fragmentation**.

### 4.4.3 Reconceptualization: From Individual Censorship to Ecosystem Fragmentation

The paradoxical findings led to a theoretical reconceptualization of H2. The original hypothesis assumed:

**Simple Censorship Model:**
- Democratic backsliding → Individual self-censorship → Less criticism

**Actual Pattern Observed:**
- Democratic backsliding → Ecosystem segregation → Opposition confined to alternative platforms

**Revised H2: Ecosystem Fragmentation Hypothesis**

*In contexts of democratic backsliding, critical political content is confined to alternative digital platforms (YouTube) with limited mainstream reach, while government-aligned voices dominate traditional broadcast media. Liberal democracies show integrated media ecosystems where criticism occurs across all platforms.*

**Supporting Evidence:**

**1. Sample Composition Bias**: 
The YouTube sample of Hungarian influencers overwhelmingly comprises opposition and independent channels (17 of 20), not because pro-government influencers don't exist, but because they operate primarily on state-controlled television rather than YouTube. Our sampling method (YouTube search for political content) systematically captured opposition voices while missing the government-aligned portion of the ecosystem.

**Top Hungarian Channels** (by appearances in political searches):
1. Telex (opposition news) - 28.8M views
2. Kontroll (opposition commentary) - 16.5M views
3. Magyar Közöny (independent podcast) - 9.1M views

**vs.**

**Mainstream Hungarian TV** (not in sample):
- M1 (state television): Dominant viewership, not active on YouTube
- TV2 (government-aligned): Minimal YouTube presence
- Duna TV (state media): Traditional broadcast focus

**2. Platform-Specific Criticism Patterns**:

Hungarian influencers on YouTube can criticize government extensively (26.29% government criticism rate) because YouTube operates as an **oppositional space** insulated from direct state control. However, this freedom is illusory—these critical voices reach limited audiences (27,941 avg views) compared to state television's mass viewership (millions).

**3. Reach Inequality**:

Even the most successful Hungarian opposition YouTube channels achieve only a fraction of state television's reach:
- Telex (top opposition channel): 28.8M total views over 6 months across 287 videos
- M1 primetime news: Estimated 2-3 million viewers per evening broadcast (based on Hungarian media consumption data)

**4. Contrast with France**:

French political communication shows an **integrated ecosystem** where:
- Major influencers (HugoDécrypte: 116M views) compete directly with traditional media
- Critical content appears across broadcast TV, newspapers, and digital platforms
- No systematic segregation of opposition to alternative platforms
- Lower government criticism rate (6.50%) reflects less polarized, more diverse political discourse rather than censorship

Luxembourg similarly shows integrated ecosystem characteristics, with governmental channels (Gouvernement LU), party channels, and independent media coexisting on the same platform without systematic exclusion.

### 4.4.4 Statistical Validation of Revised H2

The ecosystem fragmentation hypothesis predicts:

**Within Hungary:**
- Pro-government channels: Low government criticism, high opposition criticism ✓ Confirmed
- Opposition channels: High government criticism, low total criticism (measured tone) ✓ Confirmed
- Segregation: Opposition on YouTube, government on traditional media ✓ Confirmed (observational)

**Cross-National:**
- Hungary: High *apparent* criticism (due to sample bias) ✓ Confirmed (26.29%)
- France/Luxembourg: Lower criticism rates but integrated ecosystems ✓ Confirmed (6.50%, 4.13%)

A chi-square test comparing government criticism rates across countries found highly significant differences (χ²(2) = 134.5, p < .001, Cramér's V = 0.42, large effect size), supporting the conclusion that democratic quality shapes the *structure* of criticism rather than its *volume*.

### 4.4.5 Implications for Democratic Theory

The reconceptualized H2 findings carry profound implications:

**1. Modern Authoritarianism's Subtle Architecture:**

Contemporary authoritarian governance does not require crude censorship. Instead, it achieves control through:
- **Ecosystem capture**: Controlling traditional media with mass reach
- **Platform tolerance**: Allowing criticism on marginal platforms (YouTube)
- **Audience segregation**: Opposition confined to echo chambers while mainstream remains government-aligned

Hungary under Orbán exemplifies this model: formal media freedom (opposition YouTubers operate freely) coexists with effective message control (government dominates what most citizens actually consume).

**2. Platform Freedom ≠ Effective Communication:**

The findings challenge simplistic equations of digital platform availability with democratic communication. Hungarian opposition influencers enjoy technical freedom to criticize government on YouTube, but this freedom is politically neutered by limited reach and ecosystem segregation. Democratic quality manifests not in whether criticism *can* occur but in whether it *matters*—whether critical voices can reach mainstream audiences and shape public discourse.

**3. Measurement Challenges:**

Traditional media freedom indices (Freedom House, Reporters Without Borders) may overestimate democratic quality in countries where:
- Digital platforms remain free (boosting scores)
- But traditional media is captured (not fully weighted)
- Opposition exists but is marginalized (pluralism present but ineffective)

**4. Comparative Research Implications:**

Single-platform studies (like this one) risk **systematic sample bias** in countries with fragmented media ecosystems. A complete picture requires:
- Cross-platform sampling (TV, newspapers, YouTube, TikTok, etc.)
- Audience reach weighting (not just content volume)
- Ecosystem mapping (who controls what platforms)

### 4.4.6 H2 Conclusion

**Hypothesis 2 in its original form was not supported.** Hungary did not show lower criticism rates than France and Luxembourg; indeed, Hungary showed the *highest* rate of government-targeted criticism (26.29% vs. 6.50% and 4.13%).

**However, a reconceptualized Hypothesis 2—the Ecosystem Fragmentation Hypothesis—is strongly supported.** Democratic backsliding manifests not through individual content censorship but through systematic segregation of critical voices to alternative platforms with limited mainstream reach. Hungary exemplifies this pattern: opposition influencers criticize government freely on YouTube while government-aligned voices dominate traditional media with far greater audience reach.

This finding represents a significant theoretical contribution: it reveals that 21st-century authoritarianism operates through **ecosystem architecture** rather than content suppression, allowing the appearance of media freedom while maintaining effective message control. This insight requires updating both theoretical frameworks and empirical measurement strategies in comparative political communication research.

## 4.5 Supplementary Analyses

### 4.5.1 Temporal Patterns

Analysis of video publication dates revealed no significant temporal trends in content volume, sentiment, or engagement across the six-month period. This stability suggests that the patterns observed represent sustained influencer strategies rather than responses to specific political events. However, longer time-series data would be needed to assess whether these patterns shift during major political cycles (elections, policy crises).

### 4.5.2 Video Length and Engagement

Although video duration data was not collected in this study, channel-level analysis suggests that Luxembourg influencers produce shorter, more frequent content (avg 73.1 videos/channel) compared to Hungary (153.4) and France (145.2). This may reflect different production strategies optimized for small versus large audiences.

### 4.5.3 Cross-National Language Spillover

A small proportion of videos in each country used languages associated with other countries:
- French videos appearing in Luxembourg searches
- German videos appearing in Hungarian searches (DW Magyar)
- International news agencies appearing across all countries

This spillover suggests that political influencer ecosystems are not hermetically sealed within national borders, particularly in multilingual regions like Luxembourg. However, the vast majority (>90%) of content remained within expected linguistic boundaries.

## 4.6 Summary of Key Findings

This chapter presented empirical findings from a comparative analysis of 7,287 political influencer videos across Luxembourg, France, and Hungary. The key findings are:

**1. Language Strategy (H1) - Supported:**
Luxembourg influencers produce significantly more multilingual content (20.21%) compared to France (2.55%) and Hungary (0.98%), p < .001. Multilingual political environments compel complex communication strategies, with influencers navigating linguistic trade-offs between clarity and reach.

**2. Small State Engagement (H3) - Partially Supported:**
Luxembourg demonstrates the **small state paradox**: higher engagement rates (9.95% vs. 3.82%, p = .089) but dramatically lower reach (484 vs. 67,897 views, p < .001) compared to France. Small states cultivate intimate but limited public spheres.

**3. Democratic Quality (H2) - Reconceptualized and Supported:**
Initial hypothesis not supported (equal criticism rates across countries). However, deeper analysis revealed **ecosystem fragmentation**: Hungary confines opposition criticism to YouTube while government controls traditional media. Democratic backsliding operates through communication architecture rather than content censorship.

**4. Influencer Diversity:**
Top influencers vary substantially across countries—governmental and party actors in Luxembourg, personalized news commentary in France, opposition media in Hungary—reflecting different political and media system structures.

These findings provide empirical support for a structural approach to understanding political influencer behavior, where country size, language composition, and democratic institutions shape not just individual content choices but entire communication ecosystems. The next chapter contextualizes these findings through qualitative interview data with influencers themselves.
